Rising to Mr Honner's Grand Challenge

["Build and maintain a free, comprehensive, modular, and adaptable repository of learning materials for all secondary mathematics content."]
The key to realizing Mr Honners Grand Challenge is: [" A full-time staff of professional teachers, mathematicians, designers, and editors ..."] While the costs would be minor compared to the global costs of textbooks---even for a large staff---this is no guarantee of success. For instance, the Sage project---at least as grand in outlook---has failed to attact significant funding even in the face of the overwhelming sum spent on commercial software ([http://sagemath.blogspot.co.nz/2014/08/what-is-sagemathcloud-lets-clear-some.html,link]). Furthermore, even with funding, it is arguable that this is not the best model. The life of such a project will *always* be precarious --- from budget to budget --- as financial and political winds change. Better to accept, from the outset, that the success depends on grass-roots support. The question, then, is not how to attract funding---but how to organize the people that are willing to help for free. For inspiration consider the develoment of the Linux kernal. Certainly there are commercial aspects to Linux---but its continuing success has grown from its volunteer community. Furthermore, it is not a great leap to recognise in the Linux community---and the broader open-source sofware community---the same types of people that would be willing to rise to Mr Honners Grand Challenge.
The leading players are:
  • Students
  • Teachers
  • Mathematicians
  • Scientists and Engineers
  • Web developers
  • Writers
The technical challenge is to find the common ground for these six roles---where all can contribute (according to their abilities) and consume (according to their appetites).
Must haves:
  • open source
  • based on web standards
  • all browsers
  • matrix-friendly syntax
  • no server administration
  • share code among modules (blabs)
  • compute in-browser
