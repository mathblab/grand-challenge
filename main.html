Rising to Mr Honner's Grand Challenge

Build and maintain a free, comprehensive, modular, and adaptable repository of learning materials for all secondary mathematics content.
---Patrick Honner
We respond to Mr Honner's challenge with a combination of open-source project organization and web technologies. Rather than "A full-time staff of professional teachers, mathematicians, designers, and editors ..." we turn to the latent potential of volunteers, and describe how their efforts can be focused. First though, consider the problems with a conventional approach. While the costs to run such an organization would be __relatively__ inexpensive (compared to the global costs of textbooks) this is no guarantee of success. For instance, the Sage project---at least as grand in outlook---has failed to attact significant funding even in the face of the overwhelming sum spent on commercial software ([http://sagemath.blogspot.co.nz/2014/08/what-is-sagemathcloud-lets-clear-some.html,link]). Furthermore, even with funding, it is arguable whether a conventional organization is the best model. Its life will *always* be precarious --- from budget to budget --- as financial and political winds change. Better to attach, from the outset, the success of the project to its grass-roots support. The question, then, is not how to attract funding---but how to organize the people that are willing to help for free. For inspiration consider the develoment of the Linux kernel. Certainly there are commercial aspects to Linux---but its continuing success has grown from its volunteer community. Furthermore, it is not a great leap to recognise in the Linux community---and the broader open-source sofware community---the same types of people that would be willing to rise to such a Grand Challenge.
Students, teachers, mathematicians, scientists, engineers, web developers and writers should each be able to contrubute (according to their interests and abilities) and consume (according to their appetites). The technical challenge is to find the common ground. Any solutiuon should:
  • be modular;
  • work in all browsers;
  • be open source;
  • be based on web standards;
  • have a Matrix-friendly syntax;
  • require no server administration;
  • share code between contributions;
  • compute in the browser;
  • encourage exploration (not only explanation);
  • have an adaptable workflow.
